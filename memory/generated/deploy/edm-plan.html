EDM Plan

How to read this plan

This is the email direct marketing plan for Maxxim, produced in the Deploy phase against Master Briefing v2.1, the Gate 1 decisions of 27 June 2026, and the 1 July refresh that folded in the 29 June product mechanics and the live Jewell partner site. It is the execution blueprint for email: list management, automation workflows, segmentation, deliverability, compliance, and measurement. It briefs whoever stands up and runs the email programme, and it reads directly off the marketing-plan.html always-on email streams, the copy-deck.html voice and CTA library, and the customer-profile.html journeys.

Growth is the frame and the lead word. Every email Maxxim sends exists to move one of three programmes forward, and all three ladder back to the single growth motion the marketing plan sets out: recruit a founding cohort of trusted operators, equip them to sell the Growth Diagnostic to their own clients, and hold the community together so the engine compounds. The primary lifecycle audience is the trusted operator, the Client Partner. Maxxim does not email end clients at scale; end-client sequences are partner-mediated and co-sent under the partner's name and relationship.

Three email programmes, not one broadcast list:

  • Programme 1, PARTNER nurture. Recruit the founding cohort (the named pipeline and the 3 July session invitees), then onboard signed partners onto the platform and the standards. This is the near-term front door and the priority programme.
  • Programme 2, END-CLIENT nurture. Built around the $4,950 Growth Diagnostic and the move into the 3D engagement. Partner-mediated and co-sendable, so a partner can carry it to a client they already hold, under their own name.
  • Programme 3, PARTNER COMMUNITY communications. The cadence that keeps the community alive: weekly table note, monthly business review, quarterly strategy and showcase, annual offsite. This is the retention and compounding engine, not a campaign.

What the email programme markets is a real, showable engine, not a promise. Three named specialists, each a process, make the method concrete, and email uses them as proof rather than as a feature list. Max does the words in Claude Cowork (strategy, plans, copy, briefs, summaries), and Max drafts most of this programme's email copy. Sal does the pixels in the Design Studio (any header mark or proof image an email needs). Pip runs the Social Studio and is a sibling channel, not an email tool. The method is 3D plus Deepen (Discover, Design, Deploy, then the compounding loop), gated end to end, and the gates are the visible answer to the trust gap that sits under every send: can AI hit a standard I will put my name on. Email closes that gap with a gated method, AI speed, a named human, and Pottsville as the cleared proof.

The same discipline as every upstream document applies. Real anchors are stated as real: the Growth Diagnostic at $4,950 credited forward, the premium band of $25,000 to $250,000 with a recent run near $80,000, the specialist module near $1,500. Every partner economic, every list-size assumption, and every benchmark is illustrative, pending pilots, and requires Clent sign-off before it is treated as locked or used externally. Open items are carried, not invented: Maxxim's category lead word stays open, the front-door product stays open, the fractional CMO model stays open for Maxxim (it is how Jewell packages the method as a partner, not a Maxxim fact), and the 3 July session promise wording stays Clent's. Proof respects clearance: Pottsville Acupuncture is the only cleared public Maxxim example, and Jewell's own named results stay under Jewell's banner and are never re-used as Maxxim's own public proof. Ownership is Clent and Raef only. House style is locked and hard: no em dashes, no exclamation marks, Australian English, the business is Jewell and never Joule. No email goes out externally without Clent sign-off, and the sending posture is deliberately conservative: a human approves every send.

Where a list count, an open rate, or an ESP detail was not supplied, the plan states the assumption inline and keeps recommendations platform-agnostic.   • Target. A separate end-client list grown only through partners, never bought, reaching a first cohort of co-sendable contacts as partners run their first Diagnostics. Jewell, already live, is the reference for how a partner brings clients onto the motion.

  • Target. Because the list is small, named, and high-trust, hold partner-nurture open rates well above generic B2B norms: an illustrative 55 to 70 percent open and 8 to 15 percent click on recruitment nurture; community-cadence opens of 60 percent or higher. End-client nurture, co-sent by a partner the contact already trusts, should hold 45 to 60 percent opens. [Illustrative; confirm the real baseline from the first cohort sends.]

Objective 3: Revenue attribution

  • Target. Email is a support and rhythm channel near term, not the primary revenue driver, because the partner deal turns on a near-solo decision and a direct founder relationship, and the end-client deal turns on the partner relationship. The honest target is influence, not last-click revenue: email should be a confirmed touch on the majority of session registrations and on every Diagnostic booked through the co-send sequence. Revenue attribution is set against the commercial lead-source rule (partner-sourced, Maxxim-sourced, referred), not a marketing last-click model, because that tag drives the clip and the finder's fee. [No revenue percentage is invented; the cohort sets it.]

Objective 4: Automation coverage

  • Target. At least the four core flows live before the founding-cohort window opens, covering recruitment, onboarding, end-client lead nurture, and re-engagement, so that no lifecycle stage on either layer is left to manual sends alone. Triggered automations carry the repeatable rhythm; broadcasts carry only the timely, human, community moments. Consistent with the product posture, every automated send is queued for human approval before it goes, not auto-fired blind.

Objective 5: Deliverability and compliance foundation

  • Target. SPF, DKIM, and DMARC authenticated on the sending domain before the first external send; a documented warm-up; Australian Spam Act compliance on every email from send one. Deliverability is the invisible foundation: the best session invitation is worthless in a spam folder.

2. List Strategy

Acquisition sources

  • Partner list. The named founder pipeline (direct, consented at the relationship); warm operator-to-operator referral; the 3 July session registration form; LinkedIn organic inbound directed to a partner-interest form on maxxim.ai. The maxxim.ai contact form (the embedded app now live in the delivery stack) is the capture surface, feeding the ESP with consent and source logged. No purchased lists, ever.
  • End-client list. Grown only through partners. A partner adds a client they already hold, with the client's consent, via a co-send permission flow. A vertical database such as Arran's tradie list is the partner's asset and is mailed by the partner under the partner's own consent and sender identity, not merged into a central Maxxim blast.
  • Community list. A subset of the partner list: signed and onboarded partners who have opted into the cadence at the session or during onboarding.

Opt-in approach

Double opt-in for the partner list, single opt-in acceptable for warm, named, individually known contacts. Rationale: double opt-in protects deliverability and proves consent under the Spam Act, which matters most for the session form and any LinkedIn-driven sign-ups where the relationship is newer. For the named pipeline, where Clent or a partner already has an express, recorded relationship, a single confirmed opt-in with a logged consent source is defensible and lower friction. End-client co-send contacts must be double opt-in or carry a documented partner-captured consent, because the contact's primary relationship is with the partner, not Maxxim.

Segmentation model overview

High level here, detailed in Section 5. The list segments first by programme (partner, end client, community), then by door within the partner layer (license, refer, specialist), then by lifecycle stage (pipeline, registered, signed, active, lapsed). The single most important tag is the lead-source tag carried from the marketing plan, because it drives both end-client treatment and the commercial economics.

List hygiene practices

  • Hard bounces suppressed immediately and permanently.
  • Soft bounces retried to a capped count, then suppressed.
  • Inactive partner contacts (no open or click across a defined window, illustratively 90 days for the small list) routed to the re-engagement flow before any further promotional send.
  • The list is small and named, so hygiene is also relational: a contact who has gone cold is often a conversation Clent or a partner should have directly, not just an automated win-back.
  • Quarterly manual review of the partner list against the live pipeline tracker, because a named B2B list drifts through job changes and relationship shifts.

Suppression rules

  • Unsubscribes suppressed globally and immediately, honoured within the legally required window and in practice at once.
  • Hard bounces and repeated soft bounces suppressed.
  • Spam complaints suppressed permanently and flagged for review, because on a small high-trust list a complaint is a serious relationship signal.
  • Role-based addresses (info@, admin@, sales@) avoided for nurture; reach the named human, not the inbox.
  • Suppression is global across all three programmes by default, with a documented exception only where a contact has separately and explicitly consented to a different stream.

3. Email Types and Cadence

Each type below names frequency, purpose, and audience. The cadence is deliberately light and sustainable, consistent with the business plan warning that the community must stay a flywheel, not a support burden. Where the work is production rather than judgement, Max drafts the copy and a human approves before send, which is the model marketing itself.

Newsletter and community notes (Programme 3)

  • Weekly table note. A short prompt and recap around the weekly open table. Audience: signed community partners. Purpose: compounding knowledge, belonging, one shared learning.
  • Monthly business review note. Pipeline, active engagements, Diagnostics sold, and one product-roadmap line (how Max, Sal, and Pip are improving from partner friction). Audience: community partners. Purpose: performance and a direct line into the roadmap.
  • Quarterly strategy and session invitation. Audience: community partners and warm pipeline. Purpose: the case-study factory and the economics review, where illustrative numbers move toward locked.

Promotional and recruitment campaigns (Programmes 1 and 2)

  • Session recruitment sends. Invitation, reminder, and follow-up around 3 July. Audience: partner pipeline. Seasonal anchor: the founding-cohort window, July to September 2026. The session is run as partner discovery and alignment, so copy invites rather than sells, and the promise wording stays directional pending Clent.
  • Cohort offer sends. The founding-table invitation to license or refer. Audience: registered and warm operators. Economics stated in structure only, never a number.
  • End-client Diagnostic prompts. Co-sendable sequence introducing the $4,950 Growth Diagnostic. Audience: partner-held end clients, sent under the partner relationship and name.

Lifecycle emails (Programmes 1 and 2, triggered)

  • Welcome and recruit (partner), onboarding (signed partner), end-client lead nurture, Diagnostic-to-engagement, post-launch and Deepen, re-engagement. All defined in Section 4. Every triggered send queues for human approval before it fires.

Transactional emails

Transactional sends carry no marketing and are exempt from promotional suppression, but still identify the sender and respect unsubscribe on any non-essential content. 6. Content and Design Framework

Template requirements

  • Header. Minimal, Signal Blue #2D5BFF accent, Poppins, the Maxxim wordmark. On dark or Signal Blue header grounds, use the reversed logo variant so the mark holds contrast. No heavy hero imagery; the audience trusts substance over polish. Any header mark or proof image is produced by Sal in the Design Studio, off the Brand Guidelines and a locked logo.
  • Body. Single clear idea, short paragraphs, one primary CTA. Plain-text-feeling layout for partner nurture, because operator-to-operator email should read like a real person wrote it, not a marketing template.
  • CTA. One primary button or link per email, lifted from the copy-deck.html CTA library. Partner converts on "Join the founding partner cohort"; end client converts on "Book your Growth Diagnostic".
  • Footer. Sender identity (Jewell, never Joule), a physical or business address, a one-click unsubscribe, and a plain statement of why the contact is receiving this. Ownership line: Clent and Raef only. No costs anywhere.

Content guidelines per email type

  • Partner nurture. Operator-to-operator, plain, evidence-led, no hype. Short. One claim, one proof, one step. Name Max, Sal, and Pip when it makes the engine concrete, and keep them subordinate to Maxxim and never the accountable human.
  • End-client nurture. Outcome-led and reassuring, co-sent under the partner voice. Lead with the owned outcome and the named human.
  • Community notes. Warm, reciprocal, light. A real recap and a real prompt, not a polished bulletin.
  • Transactional. Plain and exact, especially the Diagnostic credit. No marketing payload, no costs.

7. Deliverability Strategy

Deliverability is the invisible foundation. The list is small and high-trust, which helps, but a cold start on a new domain can still land in spam, so authentication and warm-up are non-negotiable. Email runs on Cloudflare routing (free forwarding) or Google Workspace, per the delivery stack, with no bespoke sending build.

Authentication setup

  • SPF. A published SPF record authorising the ESP and any other legitimate senders on the sending domain. One record, correctly scoped.
  • DKIM. DKIM signing enabled at the ESP with the published public key on the domain, so every send is cryptographically signed.
  • DMARC. A DMARC record published, starting at p=none with aggregate reporting to observe alignment, then tightened to p=quarantine and ultimately p=reject once SPF and DKIM alignment is confirmed clean. Use a dedicated sending subdomain (for example, mail or send) so a deliverability issue never threatens the root maxxim.ai reputation, which the proof surface and the client site depend on.

Warm-up plan

For a new sending domain or subdomain, ramp volume gradually so mailbox providers build a positive reputation. Because the list is small, warm-up is fast but still staged.

List cleaning cadence and criteria

  • Suppress hard bounces immediately; suppress repeated soft bounces.
  • Route 90-day inactives to re-engagement; suppress from promotional sends if they do not return.
  • Quarterly manual reconciliation against the live pipeline tracker.

Engagement-based sending

Suppress low-engagement contacts from promotional and recruitment sends; keep mailing the engaged. On a relationship list this also means a cold named contact is handed to a human for a direct note rather than mailed harder. Sending to people who do not open is the fastest way to sour a young domain's reputation.

Sender reputation monitoring

  • Monitor DMARC aggregate reports for alignment and spoofing.
  • Watch bounce rate, complaint rate, and open-rate trend per send; a sudden drop signals a placement problem.
  • Use the ESP deliverability tools and, where available, a postmaster or reputation dashboard for the major providers.
  • Keep complaint rate well under the 0.1 percent threshold the major providers expect; on a small list a single complaint is a meaningful signal to act on.

8. A/B Testing Plan

Testing is constrained by list size: with a small, named list, classic split testing rarely reaches statistical significance per send, so the plan is honest about when to test statistically and when to learn qualitatively.

What to test

  • Subject lines (the highest-leverage variable, since open is the gate).
  • Preview text.
  • Send time and day.
  • CTA wording and placement.
  • Content length (short operator note versus slightly longer proof-led email).
  • Personalisation elements (named partner sender versus brand sender on end-client co-sends).

Methodology and sample sizes

  • One variable at a time, always.
  • Minimum sample. A statistically meaningful A/B subject test needs a few hundred recipients per variant to detect a typical open-rate lift at 95 percent confidence. On a list in the tens, that bar is not met, so split testing is deferred until the list crosses roughly 1,000 engaged contacts. [Illustrative thresholds; confirm against the real list and ESP calculator.]
  • Below that size, test sequentially and qualitatively. Send variant A this fortnight, variant B next, compare directionally, and weight what the founder and partners hear in conversation. Treat results as signal, not proof, and never over-read a small-sample win.
  • Significance threshold. 95 percent confidence before any winning variant is declared a standard; otherwise label it a directional learning.

Testing cadence

At least one structured learning per month, even while the list is small: pick one variable, run it sequentially, and log the result. As the end-client list grows through partner co-sends, move to true split tests on subject lines first.

9. Compliance and Privacy

Permission is non-negotiable and the programme is built around Australian law first, with GDPR considerations for any international subscriber.

Australian Spam Act 2003 compliance

  • Consent. Send only with consent, express or inferred. For the named pipeline, consent is express and recorded at the relationship. For session and form sign-ups, consent is captured at opt-in with the source and timestamp logged. Inferred consent (an existing business relationship) is used carefully and documented, never assumed for cold contacts.
  • Sender identification. Every commercial email clearly identifies Maxxim and Jewell as the sender, with accurate business details and a valid reply path. The sender is always identifiable and never spoofed.
  • Functional unsubscribe in every email. Every commercial send carries a working, low-friction unsubscribe that stays functional for at least 30 days after send.

Unsubscribe process

One-click unsubscribe, no login or survey required. Honoured immediately in practice and within the legally required five business days at the outside. Unsubscribes suppress globally across all three programmes unless the contact has separately and explicitly consented to a distinct stream.

Data handling

  • What is collected. Name, email, organisation, door or value tier, lifecycle stage, lead-source tag, engagement history, and consent record. Nothing sensitive beyond what the relationship needs.
  • Where it is stored. In the ESP and the light CRM or pipeline tracker, behind access controls; admin access limited to Clent and Raef and any explicitly authorised operator. Deliverables and records that surface on the dashboard are saved to Maxxim's secure storage.
  • Retention. Hold active-contact data for the life of the relationship; review and purge unconsented or long-inactive records at the quarterly hygiene pass. Consent records retained as proof of permission.
  • Public-sector and not-for-profit end-client data carries the additional privacy and records obligations the customer profile flags; confirm per jurisdiction.

Consent management

Opt-in preferences and source are recorded at capture and honoured at send. A contact can move stream or opt down (for example, community-only) without fully unsubscribing, recorded in the ESP. The lead-source and consent tags travel with the contact through the funnel.

GDPR considerations

For any international subscriber, apply GDPR-grade practice: a clear lawful basis (consent or legitimate interest, documented), a plain privacy notice at capture, the right to access and erasure honoured on request, and double opt-in as the default for non-Australian sign-ups. Given the partner-led, named-list model, international contacts will be rare and individually known, which makes documented consent straightforward.
